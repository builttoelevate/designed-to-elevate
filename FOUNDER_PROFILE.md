## The Fitchin story (full version)

Four years ago I started Fitchin Automotive Detailing & Window Tinting from nothing. No investors, no inheritance, no shortcut. Just an idea, the willingness to figure things out, and the pressure to make it work.

In the early days I was close to everything. I did the work in the bay. I answered the messages. I wrote the quotes. I handled the customers. I learned what people asked before booking, what made them hesitate, what made them trust a shop, and what made them choose one place over another.

The business grew. As it grew, I started noticing something: the work itself wasn't the only thing driving growth. The system around the work mattered just as much. The website, the Google presence, the service pages, the way the offer was explained, the quote process, the follow-up — all of it. A great install with a confusing website still loses leads. A good business with a weak online presence still gets passed over.

So I started learning the marketing side. The website. SEO. How customers move from search, to site, to quote, to booking. What makes them click. What makes them call. What makes them trust a shop enough to hand over their keys.

Eventually I trained my sister's boyfriend to handle the tint work and stepped out of the install side completely. That changed everything. Instead of spending my days in the bay, I moved fully into the marketing side of Fitchin — the website, the offers, the lead flow, the Google presence, the customer journey. The shop kept growing under that work. It still is.

That's the foundation Designed to Elevate is built on. Not theory. Not a course I took. The actual experience of building and marketing a local service business from the inside.

## How I think about marketing

A few principles I keep coming back to:

- **It's not a hustle problem. It's a system problem.** Most service-business owners are working hard enough. They're not lazy. They're missing structure.
- **Pretty isn't the point.** A website that looks nice but doesn't bring clarity, trust, or action is decoration. The point is to help customers understand the business and take a next step.
- **Service businesses aren't built in spreadsheets.** They're built through early mornings, customer conversations, reviews, referrals, and a thousand small decisions. Marketing has to respect that reality.
- **Most local businesses are better than their website makes them look.** That's the gap I want to close.
- **Clear beats clever.** Every time.

## Why Designed to Elevate

I didn't start this to be another marketing agency. There are plenty of those, and most of them are either too expensive, too generic, or too disconnected from how a real service business actually runs.

I started Designed to Elevate because I know the kind of business I'm helping. I've been in it. I've answered the late-night messages. I've fielded the quote requests. I've watched leads slip away because the page wasn't doing its job. I know what it feels like when your work is better than your website makes it look.

I want to give other service owners the kind of marketing system I wish I'd had when I was starting Fitchin — without the agency price tag, without the long contracts, and without the fluff.

## My voice — how I actually talk

- Direct. Plain. No hedging.
- Sentences run short. Punches land harder that way.
- I don't lean on jargon. I don't say "solutions" or "synergy" or "leverage."
- I use words a shop owner would use. "Phone ringing." "Leads slipping away." "Work better than your website makes it look." "Bay." "Quote." "Book."
- When I write about marketing, I write about it the way a service owner thinks about it — not the way an agency pitches it.
- I'd rather be plain and true than polished and generic.
- I don't oversell. I don't promise results I can't guarantee. I'd rather lose the sale than make a claim I can't back up.

### Lines that sound like me

- "Built From the Bay, Not a Boardroom."
- "It's not a hustle problem. It's a system problem."
- "More customers. Less chasing."
- "Most local businesses are better than their website makes them look."
- "I'm not coming at this from theory. I'm coming at it from inside the kind of business I help."

### Phrases I avoid

- "Proven system"
- "Guaranteed results"
- "Award-winning"
- "Industry-leading"
- "Trusted by [X]"
- "Solutions"
- "Synergy" / "leverage" / "ecosystem" / any agency buzzword stack
- Anything that sounds like a LinkedIn post
